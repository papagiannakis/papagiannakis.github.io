The chart says VR had a winter.

I was inside it. There wasn't one.

In 2002 — when most cycle charts mark the deepest point of VR's "dead years" — I was at MIRALab in Geneva with Prof. Nadia Magnenat Thalmann, deploying life-size augmented reality characters at Pompeii (LIFEPLUS — ranked #1 of 150 EU IST proposals that year) and writing some of the earliest serious VR code for medical emergency training (the JUST project).

Both shipped in 2002. Both inside what the headlines called a wasteland.

It was not a wasteland. We were rendering Roman patricians on a Pentium III with vertex shaders that hadn't existed three years earlier. We were debugging lip-sync at 25 fps. We were laying down the substrate of every medical VR product on the market today — including the platform I would later build at ORamaVR.

The chart tracks headlines, capital, product launches. It does not track substrate. And substrate is what eventually decides everything.

The 2022–2024 "metaverse winter" is the same optical illusion. Reality Labs lost $46B and the press turned — but in those exact three years 3D Gaussian Splatting matured, passthrough optics reached consumer comfort, our MAGES clinical-RCT evidence crossed statistical significance, and ChatGPT arrived with what turns out to be VR's long-missing piece: a content-production layer cheap enough to actually fill the metaverse.

ChatGPT did not eclipse VR. ChatGPT finished it.
